A Low-Carbon Energy Transition Can Save the Global Economy Trillions

It may be easier than you think

Slowing down the freight train We need to get CO2 emissions under control, and it may be easier than you think. To keep global warming within a 2 degree increase, we need to halt the rapid growth of emissions to stablize at our target of 450ppm atmospheric CO2 by 2035.

The incredible economic growth of the last 150 years has been built on fossil fuels, and their emissions

To avoid dangerous warming, we need to keep atmospheric concentrations of CO2 below 450 ppm. That requires major reductions in greenhouse gas emissions. 2/3rds of these reductions will need to come from the Energy sectors.

Required Reduction by Sector, Worldwide
Two thirds of these reductions will need to come from the energy-related sectors.

Looking at these energy sectors (?), let’s see how we can reduce the costs and increase benefits of transitioning to renewable energy so we can reach our 450 ppm goal. (This article summarizes our research in our detailed report. LINK )

Power: The transition to a low-carbon electricity system alone can save the global economy $1.8 trillion over the next 20 years.

Transport: Transitioning from an oil-based to a low-carbon transportation system could save up to $3.5 trillion depending on policy choices; a combination of innovation and policies that reduce demand work better than policies that restrict supply.

Regions that import more oil than they produce — including the United States, Europe, China, and India — can see trillions in gains to their economies over the next 20 years from reducing their oil consumption in favor of low-carbon alternatives. They can benefit by transitioning to a low-carbon economy together even if net oil producing countries do not.

Governments, not private investors, own most of the fossil-fuel assets at risk.

Global control of fossil-fuel production

The fossil-fuel value at risk from a low-carbon transition represents only 2-3% of the total value held by the owners of potentially stranded assets. Further, the largest share belongs to governments, who own over half of global fossil fuel production and control as much as 70% of oil and gas production through companies that are wholly or majority owned by governments. As governments control much of the policy that may lead to stranding, they can reduce impact and recover lost value through policy.

Their choices determine who wins and loses in the transition.

Innovation

Innovation is the development of technologies and funding schemes that allow renewable energy options to become cheaper and more practical over time through research, economies of scalle, and new business models. (Hypothetical scenario where government focuses entirely on innovation)

Taxation

Taxation is a tax on the consumption of fossil fuel products, making it more expensive and thus increasing the competitiveness of renewable energy sources. (Hypothetical scenario where government focuses entirely on taxation)

Reliance on consumption taxes may result in a net cost to the global economy.

However, countries that are net oil consumers can see an overall benefit by taxing consumption

Oil consumers can act independently of oil producers to tax oil consumption. Countries that consume more oil than they produce could see an economic boost because increased tax revenue would more than offset lost oil revenue due to higher prices. If consumers do act alone, producers are better off joining them.

Low hanging fruit

Transitioning away from coal offers the most cost-effective opportunity for a low-carbon transition

the global community can achieve approximately 80% of necessary carbon reductions by transitioning away from coal for 12% of global economic value at risk. In the power sector, the U.S. and EU are already on track for a transition with minimal economic value loss; China and India would have to find alternatives to new coal plants to minimize the cost of transition to a low-carbon economy.

eliminating only 12% of the stranded asset value...

Coal fired power generation has slowed in the developed world

To be consistent with a 450ppm limit, these develop countries need to halt their development beyond the threshold. In these mature economies, it woun’t be a big cost to stop. To meet the global goal, they can simply allow existing plants to retire naturally, and stop building new ones.

Coal-fired power plants are critical energy assets today, but by retiring plants on schedule, and halting new development, future losses will be minimal.

Developed countries can reduce their coal-fired plants with minimal costs, simply by letting their 40 and 60 year old plants retire naturally rather than refurbishing them.

Coal fired power generation is still the defacto growth model in the developing world.

To be consistent with a 450ppm limit, these developing countries need to halt their development beyond the threshold. These growing economies have a tremendous opportunity to get energy development right the first time and then reap the benefits for decades to come. Continuing on a path of Coal will result in wasteful invesment, as new coal plants will simply be competing for emissions quotas with existing plants. Current investment will be future stranded asset costs that need to be written off as plants are shut down. Those investment dollars could be used much more efficiently in alternative renewables.
